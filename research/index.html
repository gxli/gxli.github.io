Giant Molecular Filament in the Milky Way

While working at the Max-Planck Institut fuer Radioastronomie, I developed an observer’s view of star formation.

Using Galactic Ring Survey data we identified one of the biggest molecular structure in the Milky Way.

Molecular Outflow from Protostars

As a theoretician I worked on outflows from protostars. Using dimensional analysis, I tried to work out a general picture of molecular outflow. It turned out that these objects can be explained through a physical mechanism called turbulence mixing, which is commonly-seen on earth and in laboratories. The results can be found here.

Channel map of a simulated outflow. The results can be found in Turbulent entrainment origin of protostellar outflows .

Gravity in Star Formation

Star formation is complicated, but as physicists we start with simple things. The physics of gravity is simple as everybody knows about Newton’s law. But studying gravity in star formation not as easy as it seems.

The major difficulty is that in reality we need to deal with complicated structures. In a recent paper we developed a new method to estimate the importance of gravity in the Position-Position-Velocity (PPV) space.

More details about the method can be found here

A visualisation of in the Perseus molecular cloud. Blue color stands for the 13CO(1-0) emission and red color stands for the G-virial, which is a measure of the importance of gravity .

Black Hole Accretion Disks

From 2007 to 2010 I worked on black hole accretion disks. I am interested in all kinds of disks, but only had time to work on the most energetic ones. The fun part is to work on the images and movies. We found that the disks look different if we take their thicknesses into account.

Simulated images of a black hole accretion at Eddington luminosity. The image on the left is a image of a (usually-assumed) geometerically-thin accretion disk, and the image on the right is for a (realistic) geometerically-thick disk. Details can be found here .
